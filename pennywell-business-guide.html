Pennywell Enterprises: How the Business Works

What the company actually does, how a family gets scholarships, what the Scholarship Wallet is, and the numbers behind it. Context for the system you're building.

The big idea

Pennywell is a scholarship concierge. Families pay Pennywell to find and win college scholarships for their student. Your "real estate" instinct is close: Pennywell keeps a large inventory of scholarship "listings" (the Hub, 1,900+ and growing), and for each family it matches the student to the ones they qualify for, then pursues those scholarships on the family's behalf. The company makes money by helping families win money for college.

What kind of scholarships?

Your question: school, government, or private?

Almost entirely private, external scholarships: awards from companies, law firms, foundations, nonprofits, professional associations, churches, and individuals. A student can apply for these no matter which college they attend, so the money is portable and follows the student. The actual Hub bears this out: law firms (Kryder Law, Terry Bryant), corporations (AbbVie, Citizens Bank, Microsoft, Cafe Bustelo), foundations (Crohn's & Colitis, Hispanic Scholarship Fund, Goldwater), professional groups (Society of Women Engineers, ALPFA), and no-essay platforms (Bold.org, Niche, ScholarshipOwl). Almost none are internal aid tied to one specific university.

  • Private / external (the core). Companies, foundations, nonprofits, associations, churches, individuals. Portable, apply from any school. This is essentially the entire Hub.
  • School / institutional (minor). Given by a college and only usable there. Pennywell keeps a small "University-Specific" category for a student's target school, used when relevant. Not the core.
  • Government (context only). FAFSA, Pell, state grants. The intake form captures FAFSA status, Pell eligibility, and income as matching context. Families file FAFSA themselves; Pennywell does not apply for these.

Why it matters: because these scholarships are portable, the same Hub serves every client regardless of where each student goes to college. That is what makes the "one big inventory" model work, and why the database is the company's core asset. If the scholarships were school-specific, the inventory could not be reused across families.

Who pays, and what they get

Three service tiers. The difference is how much of the work Pennywell does for the family — from a weekly list they act on themselves, up to full white-glove service. All are billed monthly.

Tier 1 · Scholarship Intel

$597 / month

"The Weekly Edge." 40–50 curated scholarship matches delivered weekly (up to 200/month), each with deadline, award amount, eligibility, and a direct link, including hidden opportunities from institutional and niche sources. The family applies themselves. Pennywell does the matching; the family does the applying.

Tier 2 · Application Accelerator

$1,997 / month

"The Done-With-You Partnership." Everything in Tier 1, plus 12–15 applications submitted on the student's behalf each week, AI-assisted essays refined in the student's own voice, a professional toolkit (recommendation-letter drafts, résumé, bio), and weekly progress reporting.

Tier 3 · Full Scholarship Concierge

$3,497 / month

"The White-Glove Experience." Everything in Tiers 1 & 2, plus up to 25 completed applications per week, financial-aid appeal support, bi-weekly parent strategy calls, student interview prep, college-decision strategy, and a dedicated point of contact.

Pricing while the system is being built: existing clients (friends and family) get 50% off through about August 1, since the full automated service isn't live yet. The discount is for existing clients only — new clients always pay the full rates above. Invoicing is automated monthly by tier.

Future products (Phase 2, not now): an Essay Vault, an AI toolkit, college-funding playbooks, and corporate-sponsored webinars.

How a family gets scholarships

Your question: "How do clients get their scholarship?"

Six steps, from sign-up to seeing the results. For Application Accelerator and Full Concierge clients, Pennywell runs all six (Concierge at the highest volume). Scholarship Intel clients get steps 1–2 — the matched weekly list — then apply on their own.

What is the Scholarship Wallet?

Your question: "What is this Scholarship Wallet?" and "What stats are shown?"

It's the client-facing dashboard, the family's login page. Tracey calls it a "wallet" because it works like a bank account, but for scholarships: instead of showing money you have, it shows the money being pursued and won for your student. It's the company's main proof of value, so a family can see progress any time without waiting for a call. Here is what it shows:

  • Total scholarships applied — a running count of everything submitted for the student.
  • Total dollars applied for — the combined value of all those applications.
  • Wins and total dollars won — each scholarship awarded, with amount and date.
  • Success rate — wins divided by applications.
  • Funding gap — the family's funding goal versus dollars won so far.
  • Pending decisions — submitted applications awaiting a result, with expected dates.
  • Upcoming applications — what's scheduled in the next 14 days.
  • Documents needed — anything outstanding from the family, flagged by urgency.
  • Activity log — which scholarships were applied for, day by day.
  • Strategist notes — updates the strategist leaves for the family.

How many scholarships does a client get?

Your question: "How many scholarships does a client get?"

It's a volume game. The value isn't one perfect scholarship; it's applying to many so more come back as wins.

25–75
applications submitted per client per week
~170
actually submitted for one client (Marleigh); ~289 more matched but not yet applied
win rate
wins are a fraction of applications, tracked as success rate

A student can win and stack many scholarships (unlike a job, where you take one offer), so applying widely directly increases total funding. A done-for-you family (Application Accelerator or Full Concierge) typically has hundreds of matched opportunities, with tens of applications going out weekly, building to hundreds over the program. Wins are a portion of those, which is why high volume and good matching are the whole strategy. The goal is never a single win; it is as many as it takes to close the family's funding gap.

What the data shows today: the trackers are still mostly upstream. For the sample client, of about 483 tracked rows only roughly 170 are actually submitted (289 are matched but not yet applied), and almost no decisions have been recorded yet (close to zero confirmed wins and one rejection). Statuses are also inconsistent, with five different spellings of "submitted." That is not a performance problem; it is the visibility-and-standardization gap this system is built to fix.

Why this is becoming a system

Today the work is done largely by hand, which limits how many families Pennywell can serve. The vision is to move from a boutique service to an automated platform: the same scholarship hub and dashboards serving far more clients at once (the long-term goal is hundreds of clients). Every automation you build is what lets one strategist serve more families, which is how the business grows.
